# Dot[9] Research Notes

Dot[9] is based on a simple, cautious product hypothesis: some players may feel better when motion-heavy games include a subtle visual anchor that stays fixed to the physical screen.

Motion sickness and cybersickness are often linked to sensory conflict. In plain English, the eyes, inner ear, and body do not always agree about what is happening. In many games, the eyes see fast motion, camera turns, falling, sprinting, swimming, flying, or shake, while the body feels seated and still.

Apple's Vehicle Motion Cues use animated edge dots to reduce sensory mismatch during real-world travel. In that case, the body feels real motion while the eyes may be looking at a mostly static phone or tablet.

Games create a different mismatch: strong visual motion without matching physical movement. Dot[9] adapts the broad idea of peripheral cues, but the safest default is a stable screen-fixed rest frame rather than animated movement.

Stable visual anchors and visual rest frames may help some users by giving the visual system a fixed reference point. This is why Dot[9] prioritises low-opacity cues that do not flash, pulse, or react suddenly.

Peripheral optic flow can be a trigger for some users. Subtle edge cues may help create a steady frame around that movement, while gentle vignetting can reduce the intensity of peripheral motion.

Effects will vary between users. Some players are sensitive to rotation, others to bobbing, acceleration, field of view, camera shake, contrast, clutter, or peripheral movement. Customisation is therefore essential, and presets should be treated as starting points rather than fixed answers.

Dot[9] is science-informed, not clinically validated. It should not claim to cure, prevent, diagnose, or treat motion sickness, vertigo, migraine, vestibular disorders, or any medical condition. The safest language is "may help", "some players find", "comfort tool", "user-tunable", and "visual anchor".

## Tagline Notes

- Visual anchors for motion-heavy games.
- A steadier way to play.
- Stay grounded in fast-moving games.
- A calm overlay for game comfort.

## Feature Hierarchy

1. Stable edge dots: peripheral visual rest frame.
2. Centre anchor: fixed gaze reference for games without a stable crosshair.
3. Horizon line: anti-roll or anti-vertigo reference.
4. Vignette: reduced peripheral optic flow.
5. Counter-motion dots: research-mode, user-tunable cue movement.

## Preset Rationale

The presets are conservative starting points based on the product hypothesis, not clinically validated treatment modes.

- Gentle keeps low-opacity side dots fixed to the screen as the safest baseline.
- FPS makes the side dots slightly more visible and adds an optional centre point for games without a stable crosshair.
- Vertigo adds all-edge orientation and a subtle centre point for games with roll, falling, flying, swimming, or climbing.
- Fast Motion increases visibility for racing, parkour, wide-FOV, or rapid camera movement.
- Research modes should keep settings conservative while reserving room for future counter-motion behavior.

Players should tune from these starting points slowly because comfort triggers vary widely.
